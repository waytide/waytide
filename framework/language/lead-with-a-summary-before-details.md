# Lead with a summary, then the details

Open a response with a short summary — the conclusion, decision, or state — then the supporting detail below it. Order it so the reader can tell what **must** be read from what **may** be read: the summary carries the essential; the details are there for whoever wants them.

Be economical. Length is not thoroughness, and padding buries the signal. Say what informs the reader, and stop. Prefer a short answer that can be expanded on request over a long one that pre-empts every question.

**Why:** the reader's attention is the scarce resource. A summary-first shape lets the reader grasp the point at once and choose whether to read on, instead of mining a long response for it. Verbosity taxes the reader and hides the essential among the optional.

**How to apply:** start with the summary; put the must-read before the may-read; cut words that carry no information. Related: the communicate-plainly-name-the-thing rule and the language-is-precise-here rule.
