# **Recorded the articulation that crystallized the `language-is-precise-here` rule** — Scott singled it out as worth keeping for the record. Two points, each doing work the bare "use language precisely" premise leaves implicit. **(1) Precision is neither plainness nor formality — it is the extant term, obscured from two sides.** The precise word is the one that already means exactly this. A colloquialism imposed on the culture buries the established technical term under a vaguer everyday word (wrap→mediates, green→verified, happy-path→normal-path), and recovering that term is the work. **Dramatic jargon** — an impressive or academic term reached for to signal sophistication — obscures meaning under ceremony, and is just as damaging. One buries the term beneath the casual, the other beneath the grand; both obscure. Precision leans toward neither the plain nor the technical, only toward the term that says the thing exactly. (This corrects a first draft that framed precision as a lean toward plainness — itself an imprecision, swapping one bias for its opposite.) **(2) Imprecise words do damage, and they do it quietly.** A metaphor carries baggage the author never intended and the reader cannot unsee; an overdramatic word distorts judgment (a routine tidying reads as urgent repair); a loose synonym silently merges two concepts the design depends on keeping apart. By the time the confusion shows up in the work, its origin is a word chosen carelessly some distance back. This is now the rule's own framing (the "Precision is not formality" paragraph and the Why), sharpened to this quality. Noting it here too because it reads as the *teaching* behind the whole `language` package, not merely one rule's justification — and because this session was itself an instance of point (2): green-on-arrival, "arrange", "swept"/"dead", "Law 1"/"limit A", and "vocabulary"-the-package were each a word carrying a looser concept than the work needed, surfaced one correction at a time.
